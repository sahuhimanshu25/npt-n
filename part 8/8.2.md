Detailed Readable Notes for NPTEL Exam
In this lecture, the instructor introduces Anonymous Social Networks (ASNs) as a distinct category from traditional platforms like Facebook and Twitter. While OSNs emphasize identity and persistent connections, ASNs prioritize anonymity, allowing users to share content without revealing who they are. Popular examples include 4chan, Whisper, Secret, Yik Yak, and Wickr. These platforms emerged as users became more privacy-conscious due to incidents like Edward Snowden's revelations about surveillance (PRISM project) and cases where public posts led to real-world consequences.
The lecture focuses on Whisper as a primary case study. Whisper functions as a "confessional" app where users post thoughts, secrets, or memes anonymously. Content is often overlaid on images, and interactions occur through "hearts" (likes) or private chats. Unlike traditional networks, Whisper claims not to store persistent user identities or link activities across sessions, using PINs instead of passwords for access.
Researchers collected a large dataset in 2014 (~9 million Whispers, 15 million replies, and 1 million GUIDs — Global User Identifiers). They later collaborated with Whisper to remove GUIDs, enhancing anonymity. This data enabled analysis of how users behave when identity is hidden.
Key Findings on User Interactions:

A striking 55% of posts receive zero replies, showing many contributions go unnoticed.
If a post doesn't attract attention within the first hour, it is unlikely to gain traction later.
Engagement follows a power-law pattern: a small percentage of users drive most activity, while many contribute very little.

Network Structure Analysis:
Whisper differs significantly from Facebook and Twitter. Using graph metrics:

It has a high average degree (users interact with many others) but a low clustering coefficient (little tendency for friends-of-friends to connect).
This indicates a random graph structure where interactions occur mostly with strangers rather than tight social circles.
The assortativity coefficient near zero further confirms randomness — unlike the "small-world" phenomenon in traditional networks where hubs and clusters dominate.

Content Deletion and Moderation:

18% of Whisper content is deleted (much higher than Twitter's ~4%).
Peak deletion happens 3–9 hours after posting.
Sensitive topics like sexting, selfies, and casual chat face the highest deletion rates, reflecting user regret or platform moderation.

Geo-Spatial Patterns:

Interactions are surprisingly local: 90% of paired users are in the same state, and 75% are within 40 miles.
In smaller local populations, users are more likely to encounter and interact with each other.

Engagement Over Time:

Despite a growing user base, the daily volume of new posts remains stable.
This suggests high "churn" — new users join, post a few times, and then become inactive. Stickiness (long-term user retention) is low compared to identity-driven networks.

Overall Implications:
Anonymous networks like Whisper enable freer expression but result in more transient, random interactions and higher content deletion. They lack the persistent social ties and strong communities of traditional platforms. This has both positive aspects (privacy protection) and challenges (difficulty in building lasting engagement or accountability).
The lecture reinforces that anonymity changes user behaviour dramatically — reducing inhibition but also weakening network stickiness and community formation. Students are encouraged to think about how these dynamics affect trust, privacy, and content moderation on modern platforms.
