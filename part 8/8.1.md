Complete Study Notes: Week 8 – Profile Linking Across Social Networks
This lecture addresses a practical and technically challenging problem: how to determine whether multiple social media handles (e.g., Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn) belong to the same real-world person. The instructor uses examples from his own accounts (pam.kumuru on Facebook, punuru on Twitter, punuru on LinkedIn) to illustrate the task.
The Core Problem and Motivations
Users maintain presence on multiple platforms, often with slightly different handles or details. Linking them is useful for several reasons:

Marketing and Advertising: Avoid wasting budget by sending the same ad to the same person across platforms (e.g., 437k likes on Facebook + 153k followers on Twitter + 800k on LinkedIn may have significant overlap).
Sentiment Analysis: Prevent double-counting opinions. If one person posts positively on Facebook and the same person negatively on Twitter, the true sentiment should reflect a single user, not inflated numbers.
Law Enforcement and Security: Track malicious actors who use different handles across networks for spreading misinformation or illegal content.
Research and Personal Curiosity: Understand your own "social footprint" or verify identities.

The challenge is that users frequently change usernames, profile pictures, bios, or privacy settings. Simple string matching is often insufficient.
Approaches to Profile Linking
Several methods can be used, ranging from simple to advanced:

Self-Identification: The strongest signal. Users often explicitly link accounts (e.g., putting a Twitter handle in a Facebook bio, or sharing the same album link across platforms). This provides near-certain evidence of the same person.
Attribute Comparison:
Profile pictures (visual similarity).
Bios, locations, education, workplace.
Common links or external websites.
Posting patterns and content style.

Temporal / Historical Analysis:
Track username changes over time. Even if current handles differ, past usernames may overlap or follow similar patterns.
Monitor when profile details (picture, bio) change and correlate across platforms.

Graph-Based Features:
Compare friend/follower networks.
Look for overlapping connections or mutual friends.

Machine Learning Classification:
Extract 20–50+ features (name similarity, length, character patterns, temporal evolution, Jaccard distance for strings, etc.).
Train classifiers (e.g., SVM) to output a probability score that two profiles belong to the same person.
Historical data significantly boosts accuracy.


Key Insights from Research

Username changes are common (5–7% of users in studied datasets changed handles).
Profile pictures change frequently (up to 40% of users changed them multiple times in short periods).
Link farmers and spammers often show high in-degree/out-degree ratios near 1, mimicking normal users.
Combining current + historical data improves matching accuracy (e.g., ~76% with ML classifiers in referenced work).

Practical Takeaways
Profile linking is not always straightforward. If a user deliberately keeps accounts separate (different pictures, no cross-links, varied bios), it becomes harder. However, most users leave enough overlapping signals (pictures, links, posting style, temporal patterns) for successful matching. Companies and researchers can use these techniques, but they raise serious privacy concerns around deanonymization.
