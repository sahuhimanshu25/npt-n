This lecture continues the Privacy topic. It emphasizes that privacy is highly contextual, difficult to define universally, and increasingly threatened by self-disclosure combined with powerful technologies like face recognition.

2. Definitions of Privacy

Robert’s Definition: Privacy is complex, entangled in competing dimensions, and difficult to address uniformly.
Alan Westin’s Classic Definition (1967):
“Privacy is the claim of individuals, groups, or institutions to determine for themselves when, how, and to what extent information about them is communicated to others.”

Core Idea: Privacy = Control over personal information (balancing disclosure vs. anonymity).
3. Four Types of Privacy

Information Privacy — Most relevant to this course (online data, social media).
Communication Privacy — Telephones, emails, messaging.
Territorial Privacy — Home, city, country.
Bodily Privacy — Physical self (e.g., CCTV cameras).

4. Rising Privacy Risks in OSNs

Explosive growth of photos: From 100 billion photos worldwide (2000) → billions uploaded monthly on Facebook alone.
Companies (Facebook, Google, Microsoft, Apple) acquired face recognition firms.
Self-disclosure: Users voluntarily reveal location, activities via selfies (e.g., at Taj Mahal).
Please Rob Me website: Aggregated location data from social media to show when people were away from home.
Improving face recognition + cheap cloud computing + advanced re-identification techniques = major threat.

5. Key Research on Re-identification Attacks
Goal: Show how publicly available data + off-the-shelf face recognition can reveal sensitive information.
Experiment 1: Online vs Online (Dating Site + Facebook)

Identified dataset: ~270k Facebook profiles from one city.
Unidentified dataset: Profiles from a popular dating site (pseudonyms).
Compared >500 million image pairs using PittPatt (later acquired by Google).
Used Mechanical Turk (MTurk) for human verification (5+ Turkers per pair, Likert scale 1–7).
Result: ~10% (1 in 10) profiles could be re-identified.

Experiment 2: Offline vs Online (Campus Photos + Facebook)

Took photos of students on campus + survey.
Matched live photos against Facebook profiles in real-time.
Result: 38% successful matches.

Experiment 3: Inference Attack (Predicting SSN)

Combined face data + public profiles.
Result: First 5 digits of Social Security Number predicted correctly for 27% of subjects with just 4 attempts.

Broader Implication: Public photos + face recognition + other data sources can lead to serious privacy breaches (gender, location, SSN/Aadhaar-like numbers, etc.).
