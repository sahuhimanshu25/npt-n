2. Taxonomy of Social Media eCrimes (5:50 - 26:22)

Phishing: Traditional credential theft expanded to OSM. Users are tricked via malicious links (often mimicking platform support).
Fake Customer Service Accounts: Criminals monitor public support requests and reply using impersonation accounts.
Fake Comments/Engagement: Scammers use bot or fake accounts to comment on trending posts to spread malware/phishing links.
Fake Live Streaming: Exploits curiosity around live events by directing users to malicious landing pages.
Online Discounts & Surveys: Lures like discount codes or personality surveys to harvest data.
Fake Tips (Foursquare): Malicious links posted as venue recommendations.
Social Reputation Manipulation: Inflation of Likes, Reviews, Endorsements to deceive algorithms and users.
Clickbaiting: Misleading headlines directing users to malicious sites.
Hashtag Hijacking: Co-opting trending hashtags to inject spam or phishing.
Compromised Accounts: Hacking verified accounts to spread disinformation.
Impersonation: Creating fake accounts of individuals or organizations.
Work-from-home Scams: Promises of easy money through surveys/tasks.

Key Terminology

Phishing, Social Engineering, Hashtag Hijacking, Clickbait, Epistemic Uncertainty.


Detailed Narrative Notes for NPTEL Exam
In this opening lecture of Week 6, the instructor shifts focus to eCrime (electronic crime) specifically within the ecosystem of Online Social Media. After covering technical tools, trust & credibility, privacy, and policing, the course now examines how malicious actors exploit the same platforms for financial gain, data theft, reputation manipulation, and social disruption. The underlying theory is that social media’s design — high trust, rapid sharing, emotional engagement, and massive reach — creates unique vulnerabilities that traditional cybercrime did not have.
The lecture begins by reminding students of the course journey and then presents a broad taxonomy of OSM-specific crimes. The goal is not just to list attacks, but to understand their mechanisms, why they succeed on social platforms, and how they differ from classic internet crimes.
Phishing remains one of the most persistent threats. On social media, attackers no longer rely solely on emails. They post malicious links in comments, direct messages, or trending threads, often impersonating official support accounts. The value of social media credentials has increased dramatically because compromising a Facebook or Twitter account gives attackers access to a user’s social graph, interests, and behavioral patterns — information far more useful for targeted social engineering than a simple bank password.
Fake Customer Service Accounts represent a sophisticated evolution. Criminals actively monitor public complaints (e.g., someone tagging their bank about a login issue) and quickly reply from look-alike accounts offering “help.” Victims, already frustrated, willingly share details, believing they are talking to the real organization. This exploits the trust users place in responsive official-looking handles.
Fake Comments on Popular Posts and Fake Live Streaming leverage virality and urgency. Trending topics or live events (sports, Olympics, news) attract massive attention. Scammers inject comments with malicious links or create fake live video pages that redirect users to phishing sites. The psychology here is powerful — curiosity and FOMO (fear of missing out) drive clicks before users think critically.
Fake Discounts, Surveys, and Contests prey on greed and curiosity. Offers of “exclusive discounts” or “win cash by filling a short survey” are common lures. These often lead to data harvesting or malware installation. Location-based networks like Foursquare add another layer through Fake Tips — malicious recommendations left at real venues that blend seamlessly with legitimate user content.
Social Reputation Manipulation is a more subtle but economically significant crime. Fake reviews on e-commerce sites, bought likes and followers, and artificial endorsements distort perceived credibility. In an attention economy, social proof (likes, shares, ratings) heavily influences decisions. Manipulating these metrics allows fraudsters to boost products, spread misinformation, or damage competitors.
Clickbaiting and Hashtag Hijacking exploit platform algorithms and human psychology. Clickbait uses sensational headlines to drive traffic to malicious sites. Hashtag hijacking involves inserting unrelated (often promotional or malicious) content into trending conversations (#Rio2016, disaster hashtags, etc.), polluting information streams and reducing trust in the platform.
Compromised Accounts and Impersonation erode epistemic trust — users’ ability to know what is real. A hacked verified account spreading fake news (e.g., the White House explosion tweet) can cause market panic or social unrest. Impersonation of individuals or organizations further blurs authenticity.
Finally, Work-from-Home Scams (often spread via images on Pinterest or similar platforms) promise easy income through surveys or tasks, ultimately leading to data theft or financial fraud.
Theoretically, these crimes thrive because social media amplifies social engineering — attacks that exploit human psychology (trust, urgency, greed, curiosity, fear) rather than purely technical vulnerabilities. The lecture sets the stage for deeper analysis in subsequent parts, including how researchers collect and study such crime data using the same technical tools (APIs, databases, text analysis) taught earlier in the course.
