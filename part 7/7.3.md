Detailed Readable Notes for NPTEL Exam
In this final part of Week 7, the instructor explains semantic attacks, a category of cyber threats that target how humans understand and interpret information rather than breaking technical systems. Bruce Schneier classified attacks into three types: physical (attacking hardware), syntactic (exploiting software flaws like buffer overflows or DDoS), and semantic (tricking people by manipulating meaning). Semantic attacks succeed because they exploit the gap between what the user thinks is happening (mental model) and what is actually happening (system model). The larger this "semantic barrier," the easier it is to deceive someone.
The main focus is social phishing — using publicly available personal information from social networks to make phishing emails or messages appear legitimate and trustworthy. Unlike generic phishing ("Update your bank account"), social phishing feels personal because it references real details about the victim (friends, interests, events).
The lecture discusses a landmark Indiana University study from 2005. Researchers harvested public data from blogs and social networking sites of university students. They then sent spoofed emails that appeared to come from the victim's known contacts within the university. When victims clicked the link, they were taken to a fake login page that captured their university credentials.
Key Results:

Success rate jumped to 72% when the email came from a "known" social contact (experimental group) compared to only 16% from an unknown sender (control group).
70% of successful logins happened within the first 12 hours, showing the importance of fast response and takedown.
Victims often tried authenticating multiple times (sometimes dozens) when shown error messages like "server overloaded," believing it was a technical glitch rather than a scam.
Demographics: Females were more vulnerable overall. Attacks from the opposite gender were more successful. Younger students (freshmen) fell for it more than seniors. Science students showed the biggest gap between control and social conditions, while technology students were slightly more resistant.

The study highlighted how easily public social data can be weaponized. Even small details (names, interests, mutual friends) make the attack feel authentic, dramatically increasing success rates.
Conclusions and Countermeasures:

Rapid takedown of malicious sites is critical because most victims act quickly.
Education campaigns are essential to raise awareness.
Digitally signed emails and better browser protections can help.
Users should be cautious about sharing personal information publicly on social networks.

This lecture ties together many course themes: privacy risks from self-disclosure, re-identification using multiple data sources, and the need for better tools and awareness to protect users.
