omplete Study Notes: Week 7 – Link Farming and Spam Characteristics on Twitter
This lecture continues the discussion on link farming from the previous week. It uses data analysis to understand how spammers and link farmers behave, how they increase their influence, and why distinguishing them from normal users is difficult.
Recap and Core Concepts
The instructor revisits the idea of link farming — artificially creating many connections (follows) to boost an account’s visibility on Twitter. Twitter works like a search engine: accounts with higher follower counts (in-degree) appear more prominently in search results. Spammers exploit this by following large numbers of users, hoping for follow-backs to quickly grow their own follower count.
A key graph shows spam follower node rank (x-axis, log scale) versus fraction of reciprocated links (y-axis). The top 100,000 spam follower accounts account for about 60% of all links acquired by spammers. This means high-ranked spam accounts are very likely to follow back, which spammers use to their advantage.
Response Patterns and In-Degree Analysis
Another graph examines how likely users are to follow back based on their own follower count (in-degree). Users with low follower counts rarely reciprocate. As in-degree increases, the probability of following back rises significantly. Spammers target active users with decent follower counts because they are more likely to respond.
Characteristics of Link Farmers
The study looked at the top 100,000 link farmers:

Their account bios often mention topics like internet marketing, social media management, "will follow back," entrepreneurship, etc.
Many are real, active accounts — not purely fake bots.
Surprisingly, even verified and popular accounts (politicians, celebrities, brands) sometimes engage in similar behaviour to grow reach.

In-degree vs Out-degree Distribution:

Link farmers show very high in-degree (followers) and high out-degree (following) compared to random users and pure spammers.
The ratio of in-degree to out-degree for many link farmers clusters near 1 (they follow roughly as many as follow them), making them look like normal, active human users.

This similarity in behaviour makes automatic detection challenging.
Conclusions from the Analysis

Link farmers successfully inflate their social capital (perceived influence and reach).
Top link farmers are often real accounts involved in marketing or self-promotion rather than purely malicious spam.
The probability of reciprocity is a powerful tool spammers use.
Distinguishing genuine growth from artificial link farming is difficult because the patterns overlap with normal user behaviour.

Overall, the lecture shows that link farming is widespread, benefits both spammers and some legitimate users seeking visibility, and undermines the fairness of Twitter’s ranking and recommendation systems. This connects back to earlier topics like reputation manipulation and the challenges of trust on social media.
