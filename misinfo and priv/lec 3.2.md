Detailed Study Notes: Privacy Concepts and Perceptions in Online Social Media (Week 3 Continued)
In this part of the Week 3 lecture, the instructor shifts focus from trust and credibility to privacy while giving important practical advice on API usage. He strongly warns students not to share their API Key, API Secret, or Access Token with anyone. These credentials act as your digital identity for data collection. If leaked on forums or shared, others can use them to collect data in your name, which can lead to misuse, account suspension, or serious security issues. A dedicated tutorial on Twitter API is scheduled this week, following the same hands-on approach as the Facebook API tutorial.
The lecture then defines privacy as a highly contextual and subjective concept. What feels private in one situation (e.g., with friends in class) can be completely different at home, while watching a movie, or out with friends. There is no single universal definition of privacy.
A major part of the lecture covers the pioneering work of Prof. Alan Westin, who studied privacy attitudes for over 30 years. He classified people into three categories based on their privacy preferences:

Fundamentalists: People with very strong, rigid privacy expectations. They are extremely protective and unwilling to share personal information even in normal situations (roughly 25% of US citizens in his studies). They might refuse to appear in group photos.
Pragmatists: The largest and most situational group. They weigh the benefits versus risks and share personal data only when they feel the reward is worth it (e.g., sharing details for a useful service or a good discount).
Unconcerned: People who have very low privacy sensitivity and are willing to share even sensitive information (like passwords) for small incentives.

The instructor connects this to the evolution of the internet. An old mid-1990s cartoon illustrated the idea that “On the internet, nobody knows you’re a dog.” At that time, true anonymity seemed possible. However, today platforms like Google, Facebook, and Twitter can easily build detailed user profiles by combining browsing behavior, traffic data, and social media activity. This makes true anonymity very difficult.
To understand privacy perceptions specifically in the Indian context, the lecture presents findings from a large-scale national study. This study is one of the biggest privacy surveys globally, involving 10,427 respondents (collected through surveys at malls, railway stations, hotels, and some online responses), covering most states in India. It included 83 questions and provides rich, generalizable insights.
Key findings highlighted from the study (especially related to Online Social Media):

When asked about privacy of personal information on platforms like Facebook:
42% believed that setting proper privacy controls makes their data completely secure.
Around 19% said privacy is “not a concern at all.”
Many others expressed concern but still continue sharing personal information.
On accepting friend requests:
27% of respondents accepted requests from people of the opposite gender.
10.12% accepted requests simply because the person had a “nice profile picture.”
A small percentage accepted anyone or strangers with mutual friends.

These results show a clear gap between awareness and actual behavior. Many users overestimate the protection offered by privacy settings and often make risky decisions based on superficial factors. The instructor encourages students to explore the full report and dataset (publicly available) and to actively discuss various privacy issues they face on social media in the course forum.
This lecture keeps the content relatively light to allow time for hands-on API tutorials, with more privacy topics planned for future weeks.

Key Terminology

API Credentials: API Key, Secret, and Access Token — sensitive authentication details for programmatic access.
Privacy Perceptions: Individual attitudes toward sharing personal data, which vary by context and personality type.
Alan Westin’s Categories: Fundamentalists, Pragmatists, Unconcerned.
Personally Identifiable Information (PII): Data that can identify an individual.
