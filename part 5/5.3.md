Detailed Narrative Notes for NPTEL Exam
In this part of Week 5, the instructor moves from broad policing adoption to a deeper, research-oriented analysis of how police organizations and citizens communicate on Facebook pages. The central theme is whether social media can serve as a rich source for understanding human behaviour in the policing context. Rather than just counting likes or posts, the lecture emphasizes quantifying subtle attributes of communication — how people express themselves, engage with each other, show emotions, and orient their thoughts socially and cognitively.
The core research question is: Can we explore the feasibility of social media in quantifying attributes of communication (citizen-to-citizen, police-to-citizen, citizen-to-police, and police-to-police) on official police Facebook pages? This involves studying topical characteristics (what people talk about), engagement patterns (how actively they interact), emotional exchanges (positive/negative feelings and intensity), and cognitive & social orientation (how people think and relate to others linguistically).
To answer these, the team collected a large dataset from 85 official Indian police Facebook pages spanning 2010 to April 2015. This yielded over 47,474 wall posts and status updates. They distinguished between posts initiated by police and those started by citizens, and carefully tracked interactions (comments and likes) in each case. This distinction is important because it reveals asymmetries in how information flows and how discussions evolve depending on who starts them.
For topical characteristics, they used n-gram analysis (unigrams for single words, bigrams for pairs) and clustering techniques. When police initiate discussions, the topics tend to be more focused and institutional — advisories, safety campaigns, procedural updates, and awareness drives. In contrast, citizen-initiated posts are far more diverse and reactive, covering neighborhood problems, complaints, requests for action, appreciation, missing persons reports, and personal grievances. This pattern makes intuitive sense: police posts are often planned campaigns, while citizens use the platform primarily to seek help or voice concerns.
Engagement analysis revealed interesting dynamics. Police-initiated posts generally attract higher overall interaction, but when police reply to citizen complaints, the average number of comments per thread tends to be lower. This happens because police responses often provide solutions, acknowledgments, or directions that “close the loop” of the discussion. Citizen-only threads, on the other hand, can remain open longer with more back-and-forth among citizens. This highlights how official intervention can efficiently resolve conversations but may also limit deeper community discussion.
Emotional exchange was studied using tools like LIWC (Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count), which categorizes text into psychological dimensions. Citizen-initiated threads showed higher negative affect, anger, and arousal — understandable since people typically post when they face problems. However, when police actively engage in these threads, anxiety levels tend to decrease, suggesting that visible official attention provides reassurance and closure. Valence (overall positivity/negativity) and arousal (emotional intensity) thus become measurable indicators of public sentiment.
On the cognitive and social orientation side, pronoun analysis (e.g., frequency of “I”, “we”, “they”) showed that citizen posts are often self-oriented (“I have this problem…”), reflecting personal experiences. Police language, conversely, tends to be more collective and procedural.
Why This Matters (Theoretical Implications)
These analyses go beyond simple metrics. They support the idea of “communicative policing” or “community sensing,” where social media acts as a real-time sensor of public mood, safety concerns, and expectations. By systematically studying these interactions, police organizations can move from reactive to more proactive and empathetic policing. The data can serve as an early warning system, help predict emerging issues, improve resource allocation, and even measure changes in public trust over time. Ultimately, the lecture argues that thoughtful analysis of such public digital interactions can lead to safer cities through better police-citizen collaboration and data-informed decision making.
Technologically, this opens doors for building tools that automatically highlight high-priority actionable posts, suggest response templates, track sentiment trends, and support predictive analytics for policing.
